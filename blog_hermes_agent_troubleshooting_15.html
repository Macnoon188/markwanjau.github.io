Building My Own AI Assistant With Hermes Agent

The troubleshooting journey nobody talks about

Published: Monday, 27 April 2026  ·  Author: Mark Andrew Wanjau  ·  8 min read  ·  Tags: AI · Hermes · Telegram · Self-Hosted · Homelab

One thing I have learned from building homelabs and cybersecurity projects is that the exciting part is rarely the installation.

Most documentation makes it look simple: clone a repository, run a few commands, configure a few files, and everything magically works.

Reality is usually very different.

🤔 Why I Started Exploring Hermes Agent

I wanted to experiment with a self-hosted AI assistant using Hermes Agent — not only to understand how AI agents work but also to learn the infrastructure behind them.

How do they communicate with external services? How are configurations managed? How do integrations like Telegram actually connect to the AI backend?

What started as a simple deployment quickly became a lesson in troubleshooting.

⚡ The First Installation Attempt

My initial approach was straightforward. Clone the repository, install the required dependencies, configure the environment, and launch the application.

But somewhere along the troubleshooting process, things started to go wrong.

In trying different fixes, I ended up modifying parts of the installation that were originally working. Some Git files became incomplete, the repository state became inconsistent, and I reached a point where I had to stop and ask an important question:

Am I fixing the problem, or am I creating new ones?

That was probably the most valuable lesson of the entire process. Sometimes the fastest way forward is not another command. It is stepping back, understanding what changed, and returning to a known good state.

🔬 Learning to Troubleshoot Methodically

Instead of randomly applying fixes from forums and documentation, I started approaching the problem like a real system failure.

// The questions I started asking

01

What was the last known working state?

02

What changed after that?

03

Is this an application problem, a dependency problem, or a configuration problem?

04

Are the required services actually running?

This changed the entire troubleshooting process.

The goal was no longer just "make it work." The goal was to understand why it was failing.

"The goal was no longer just to make it work. The goal was to understand why it was failing."

⚙️ The Hidden Importance of Configuration Files

One of the biggest discoveries was the role of the .env file.

Many modern applications separate their code from their configuration. The application may be installed correctly, but without the right environment variables, API keys, and integration settings, it still will not function as expected.

While investigating the Hermes installation, I located the environment configuration and verified the Telegram integration section. Finding entries such as:

Telegram Bot Token
Integration parameters
Service settings

showed that the application already had the structure required for external communication.

The issue was not that Hermes lacked the feature. The issue was making sure the configuration matched the environment.

✈️ Bringing Telegram to Life

The real milestone came when the Telegram integration finally worked.

After all the troubleshooting, checking files, and reviewing configurations, I sent a message to the bot and received a response.

A message sent.

A response received.

But that moment represented several hours of understanding the system behind the scenes.

That is one of the things I enjoy most about building a homelab. Every successful service represents dozens of small lessons that never appear in a quick installation guide.

📐 What This Project Taught Me

📄

Documentation is a starting point, not a guarantee. Every environment is slightly different. Real deployment always involves adapting.

⚙️

Configuration issues are often harder than installation. The app being installed doesn't mean the app is running.

🔄

A broken environment can be faster to rebuild than repair. Know when to stop patching and start clean.

🧠

Understanding the system is more valuable than following commands. Blind execution only works until it doesn't.

🎯

Small successes are more satisfying when you understand how you reached them. The struggle makes the milestone real.

🔭 What Comes Next

Now that Hermes is operational, the foundation is there:

  • A running AI agent
  • A working Telegram communication channel
  • A better understanding of the application's architecture

The next challenge is customising the agent, expanding its capabilities, and exploring how it can assist with real IT and cybersecurity workflows.

💭 Final Thoughts

One thing I appreciate about homelabs is that they remove the fear of failure.

You can break things. You can rebuild them. You can experiment with technologies you would never touch in a production environment.

The goal is not to have a perfect lab. The goal is to become the kind of engineer who knows how to recover when things stop working.

If you have built your own AI agents or self-hosted services — what was the troubleshooting lesson that taught you the most?
