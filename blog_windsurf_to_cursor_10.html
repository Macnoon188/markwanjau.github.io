From Windsurf to Cursor: Why I Switched Code Editors for React Native Dev

Published: Monday, 23 March 2026 • Author: Mark Andrew Wanjau

When I started working more seriously on my React Native projects, I spent a lot of time thinking about workflow.

Not just “what code am I writing?” but “how am I writing it?”.

The tools you use shape how you think, how fast you build, and honestly, how much you enjoy the process.

I first worked with Windsurf, and for a while, it felt impressive. It was smart, fast, and genuinely helpful.

But somewhere along the way, I realized I needed something that felt more natural, more stable, and better suited for how I work.

That is when I moved to Cursor.

And I am glad I did.

Windsurf Was Good… But Something Felt Off

Let me be fair. Windsurf isn’t a bad editor. It actually helped me learn, experiment, and ship things faster.

But as my projects grew and I wanted cleaner structure, deeper control, and fewer “surprises”, Windsurf started feeling a bit noisy.

Sometimes the AI did too much.

Sometimes it felt like it was coding for me instead of coding with me.

And sometimes, it simply got in the way.

For a creative developer experience, that matters.

Cursor Felt Different The Moment I Tried It

Cursor does not try to impress you with flashy features. It just quietly works.

And sometimes, that is exactly what you need when you are building real applications.

What made the difference for me:

✔ The AI feels collaborative instead of intrusive
✔ The editor is clean and stable
✔ Code suggestions feel grounded in context
✔ It respects your structure instead of rebuilding everything

Cursor feels like having a focused teammate sitting next to you, not a hyperactive assistant trying to rewrite your entire project every two minutes.

Why This Matters for React Native

React Native development is not just typing code.

It is structure. State management. Performance considerations. Debugging. Testing.

If your environment feels chaotic, your thinking becomes chaotic.

Cursor helped me:

• Stay organized
• Think in components and flows
• Make better architectural decisions
• Enjoy the process more

Instead of wrestling with tools, I could actually focus on building.

A Small Personal Observation

Switching tools might sound like a small decision.

But it is part of growth.

It means you are paying attention to what works for you instead of forcing things to fit.

For me, Cursor represents a calmer, more intentional development process.

It reminds me that productivity is not only about speed. It is also about clarity.

Where I Am Now

I am still learning. Still improving. Still experimenting with better ways to build React Native apps.

Cursor just feels like the right companion for this phase of my journey.

It keeps things simple. It keeps me focused.

And honestly, it makes coding feel enjoyable again.

If you are a developer, have you ever switched tools because your workflow evolved?

And if you have used AI coding editors, which one do you feel actually helps — rather than takes over?
