Bot Creation Phase 1 — Part 2

Teaching Jaspa to Think Like an IT Engineer

Published: Monday, 4 May 2026  ·  Author: Mark Andrew Wanjau  ·  8 min read  ·  Tags: AI · OpenClaw · Jaspa · Telegram · IT Support

In Part 1, I focused on getting the foundations in place.

The OpenClaw gateway was running. Ollama was connected. Telegram was configured. Most importantly, Jaspa could receive and respond to messages.

That might sound like a small milestone, but anyone who has worked with self-hosted AI tools knows that getting multiple services talking to each other is often half the battle.

Still, there was a problem. Jaspa could answer questions, but it wasn't really acting like an IT support assistant. It was acting like a generic language model. And that's where the next phase began.

🧠 The Difference Between an AI Model and an AI Assistant

One thing I learned early is that installing an AI model doesn't automatically give you an assistant.

🤖 A Model

Can generate text

Responds to any question

No defined role or behaviour

Intelligence without direction

✅ An Assistant

Has a defined purpose

Operates within a context

Follows structured behaviour

Intelligence applied to a workflow

Without guidance, Jaspa would answer questions the same way any publicly available chatbot might. Sometimes the responses were useful. Sometimes they were too broad. Other times they completely missed the role I wanted the system to perform.

That wasn't a failure of the model. It was a reminder that intelligence without context isn't particularly helpful.

🎯 Defining Jaspa's Purpose

Before making any changes, I had to answer a simple question: what exactly is Jaspa supposed to be?

I wasn't trying to build another general-purpose chatbot. I wanted an assistant that could help with:

IT support troubleshooting
Infrastructure questions
System administration tasks
Homelab guidance
Basic cybersecurity concepts

In other words, I wanted something that reflected the kind of work I already do and the direction I want my career to continue moving toward.

Once that became clear, every decision afterwards became much easier.

🎭 Building a Personality

One of the most interesting parts of the process was creating Jaspa's personality — not personality in the sense of jokes or casual conversation, but personality in the sense of behaviour.

How should it respond? Should it give direct answers? Should it ask follow-up questions? Should it explain its reasoning? How technical should it be?

These decisions have a huge impact on how useful an assistant becomes.

I quickly realized that a good IT support assistant shouldn't immediately jump to conclusions. It should gather information first. The same way a real engineer would.

🔧 Thinking Like a Technician

When users describe a problem, they're often describing symptoms rather than causes. For example:

// Symptom vs Root Cause
SYMPTOM

"My internet isn't working."

POSSIBLE CAUSE

DNS resolution failure

POSSIBLE CAUSE

Firewall rule blocking outbound traffic

POSSIBLE CAUSE

Failed network interface or cable

POSSIBLE CAUSE

Routing table misconfiguration

POSSIBLE CAUSE

ISP or upstream service outage

A good technician doesn't guess. They investigate.

That became one of the principles I wanted Jaspa to follow. Instead of immediately suggesting solutions, the goal was to encourage structured troubleshooting: ask questions, collect evidence, then suggest actions.

"At first, I thought I was teaching an assistant. In reality, I was documenting my own troubleshooting habits."

💡 Lessons From the Process

One thing that surprised me was how much this project mirrored real-world IT work. Every instruction I added forced me to think about how I approach problems.

What information do I ask for first? What assumptions should I avoid? How do I explain technical concepts to someone with less experience?

Building Jaspa became an exercise in understanding my own workflow.

In trying to make an AI think like an engineer, I had to articulate what that actually meant — and that turned out to be just as valuable as the technical work itself.

📊 Current Status — End of Phase 1

At the end of this phase, Jaspa had evolved significantly from where it started:

Working Telegram interface
Functioning AI backend
Defined behavioural guidelines
Clear purpose and role
Foundations of an IT support workflow

It still wasn't finished. But it was beginning to feel less like a chatbot and more like an assistant.

🔭 What's Next

The next step is expanding Jaspa's capabilities beyond simple conversations:

  • Structured ticket generation
  • Knowledge base integration
  • Troubleshooting workflows
  • Homelab-specific guidance
  • Security-focused responses
  • Context retention and memory

The long-term goal isn't to replace engineers. It's to create a reliable assistant that helps engineers work more efficiently.

💭 Final Thoughts

One thing I've learned from this project is that building an AI assistant isn't really about AI.

It's about understanding people, processes, and problem-solving. The model provides the intelligence. The real challenge is teaching it how to use that intelligence in a useful way.

Jaspa is still a work in progress, but every configuration change, every test message, and every improvement brings it one step closer to becoming the kind of assistant I originally imagined.

And honestly, that's been one of the most rewarding parts of the journey.
