Monitoring pfSense with Wazuh

A Step-by-Step Guide

Published: Monday, January 26, 2026 • Author: Mark Andrew Wanjau

When I decided to rebuild my homelab, I knew I wanted to stop treating cybersecurity like a “tools project” and start treating it like a real network. That meant starting with something many learners ignore — the firewall.

pfSense quickly became the backbone of everything I was building. It helped me shape traffic, create structure, and understand what was actually moving across my network instead of guessing. But having a firewall is one thing. Seeing what it is doing is another.

That is where Wazuh came in.

I wanted more than green check marks and “things look okay.” I wanted visibility. If something strange happened, I wanted to know. So I integrated pfSense logs into Wazuh and treated my lab like a small SOC would.

This guide walks through how I set it up, what I learned, and why it completely changed how I view homelabs.

What I Wanted To Achieve

My goal was simple. I wanted Wazuh to:
• Receive pfSense logs
• Turn important events into alerts
• Help me understand traffic and security behavior
• Show what attackers, scanners, or misconfigurations look like

Nothing fancy. Just meaningful visibility.

Step 1: Make Sure the Basics Are Ready

Before touching anything, I already had:
• pfSense installed and working as my firewall
• Wazuh Manager running in my lab
• Reliable connectivity between pfSense and Wazuh

If you are doing this yourself, make sure these exist first. It saves frustration.

Step 2: Configure pfSense To Send Logs

Inside pfSense, I navigated to:
Status → System Logs → Settings

I enabled remote logging and pointed pfSense to my Wazuh server’s IP address. The idea is simple — pfSense generates logs, but Wazuh helps make sense of them.

This was the moment pfSense stopped being “just a router” and became a system that told the story of what was happening in my network.

Step 3: Let Wazuh Listen

On the Wazuh side, I made sure it could receive syslog data and categorize it properly. Once logs started flowing, firewall events, blocked traffic, and connection attempts appeared in my dashboard.

That moment changed everything.

It is one thing to secure a system. It is another to actually see how it behaves.

What I Started Seeing

Traffic was no longer invisible. I started seeing:
• Blocked connection attempts
• Port scans
• Failed access attempts
• Network noise I never knew existed

The firewall felt alive. Instead of trusting it blindly, I could verify what it was doing. That clarity builds confidence.

What This Taught Me

This setup taught me something I would not have learned just by reading.

Security is not about having tools.
It is about understanding what they are telling you.

Logs are conversations.
pfSense speaks.
Wazuh listens and explains.

What I Plan To Improve Next

This is not finished. It is only step one. Next, I want to:
• Build better alert rules
• Tune noise and focus on meaningful events
• Map activity to attacker behavior
• Use this setup for real learning, not just dashboards

I want my homelab to feel like a small SOC where every tool has a purpose.

If You’re Building Something Similar

If you are learning cybersecurity, don’t skip the firewall stage. Don’t rush to flashy tools. Start with the network. Understand the flow.

If you have ideas or improvements I should try, I am always open to learning. That is the whole point of this journey.

What would you improve or add next if this was your homelab?
